# Manuscript Draft: Introduction

## Introduction

Artificial intelligence and machine-learning systems are increasingly embedded in learning environments as tutors, coding assistants, feedback generators, and adaptive practice systems. These tools can provide rapid explanations and individualized support, but their educational value depends on more than immediate helpfulness. A learner who completes a task with extensive AI assistance may not be better prepared to complete a related task independently. For this reason, the central design problem is not whether AI can help, but how assistance should be structured so that it produces durable learning rather than dependency.

Scaffolding theory provides a useful foundation for this problem. Instructional scaffolds are temporary supports that help learners perform tasks they could not yet complete alone. Effective scaffolding should eventually fade as competence increases. In human-AI learning systems, however, scaffolds are often available continuously, delivered without sufficient evidence of learner need, or optimized for answer completion rather than independent recovery. This creates a risk of overreliance, where learners become better at obtaining help than at transferring knowledge.

This paper argues that best practices for machine-learning scaffolding should be organized around learner independence. A well-designed scaffold policy should decide when to intervene, how much help to provide, whether the learner should reflect before receiving more support, and when assistance should fade. These decisions should be auditable and grounded in observable learner evidence, such as attempts, confidence, cognitive load, prior scaffold use, and task performance.

The project contributes a practical framework for adaptive ML scaffolding, an executable scaffold-policy architecture, metrics for calibration and overreliance, and a reproducible synthetic experiment for validating the study design before human-subject data collection. The synthetic experiment compares no-AI control, unrestricted AI assistance, static scaffolding, adaptive scaffolding, and adaptive timing-and-fading policies. The purpose of the synthetic layer is not to claim empirical effects on human learning, but to define and test the experimental structure, metric behavior, and reproducibility pipeline.

The guiding claim is that effective ML scaffolding should not maximize assistance. It should maximize appropriately timed independence. This distinction matters because immediate task accuracy may rise even when transfer, calibration, and unsupported follow-up performance remain weak. A publishable evaluation of AI-supported scaffolding should therefore prioritize transfer, metacognitive calibration, reduced overreliance, and independent recovery after scaffolds are removed.
